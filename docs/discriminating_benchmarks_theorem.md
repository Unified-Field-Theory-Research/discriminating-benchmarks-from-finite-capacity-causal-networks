# Discriminating Benchmarks Theorem

## Target

Construct a finite, network-native discriminating benchmark interface
downstream of the closed Paper 13 external physical evidence intake theorem.

The target is a conditional theorem only. It should eventually specify how
benchmark records can be stated, audited, and compared without silently
turning benchmark definition into physical validation, prediction success,
falsification success, or unified-field promotion.

## Closed Rungs

- `DBM-001`: Upstream binding and claim-boundary scaffold through the frozen
  Paper 13 final conditional certificate.

## Active Rung

- `DBM-002`: Define finite discriminating benchmark records.

Required boundary for `DBM-002`:

- finite benchmark identifiers
- finite target labels
- finite comparator labels
- finite regime labels
- finite observable labels
- finite expected-output descriptors
- finite audit-status descriptors
- no benchmark recovery claim
- no prediction success claim
- no falsification success claim
- no physical promotion
- no physical validation
- no empirical adequacy claim
- no observed-catalog recovery claim
- no simulation-only or fit-only shortcut
- no unified field theory claim

## Planned Rungs

- `DBM-003`: finite benchmark target, comparator, and regime descriptors
- `DBM-004`: finite benchmark outcome, uncertainty, and audit-status
  descriptors
- `DBM-005`: Paper 13 evidence-intake compatibility without validation
- `DBM-006`: benchmark stability and coarse-graining behavior
- `DBM-007`: no-hidden-promotion, validation, prediction, or falsification
  import audit
- `DBM-008`: final conditional certificate

## Residual Risk

The main risk is category error: confusing a benchmark-definition interface
with evidence that the program has passed those benchmarks. Guards must keep
benchmark record construction separate from prediction success, falsification
closure, physical validation, and physical promotion.
